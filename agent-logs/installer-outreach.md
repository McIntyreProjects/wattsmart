## Part 1: Installer Persona Profiles

### Persona A — The Sole Trader Solar Specialist

**Size:** 1 person, sometimes a subcontracted labourer for larger jobs. Operates a van, works within a ~30-mile radius. Turnover ~£120k–£200k/yr.

**Typical setup:** MCS-certified, usually RECC member. Has been in solar since the FiT era. Builds reputation through word of mouth and Google reviews. No marketing budget. May have a basic website or just a Facebook Business page.

**Pain points:**
- Feast-and-famine pipeline. Works flat out in summer, quiet in winter.
- Wastes hours answering tyre-kicker enquiries via Checkatrade or MyBuilder — people who just wanted a rough number.
- Doesn't want to chase invoices or deal with payment disputes.
- Worries about being undercut on price by bigger companies quoting unrealistically low.
- ICO/GDPR-anxious — has had a complaint about cold-calling from a list before.

**What would make them sign up:**
- "Zero risk" framing: only pays a fee when selected, never upfront. This removes the biggest objection.
- Emphasis that leads are pre-qualified (homeowner has filled out a proper brief, not just "how much is solar?").
- Simple signup — they hate paperwork. The 5-step form must feel lighter than their MCS renewal.
- Seeing the fee structure clearly upfront (5% deposit + 5% balance, min £75).

**What would make them churn:**
- Receiving leads outside their area or product scope.
- Being outbid every time and not understanding why (need win-rate feedback).
- Slow cert verification holding up their account (beyond 1 working day).
- Feeling anonymous and unsupported — no human contact number.

### Persona B — The Multi-Tech Regional Company

**Size:** 6–25 fitters. Covers solar, battery, ASHP, sometimes EV chargers. Annual turnover £1M–£5M. Has a dedicated office manager and possibly a sales/admin person.

**Typical setup:** MCS, RECC, HIES, NAPIT, possibly TrustMark. Likely on Trustpilot with 50+ reviews. Has a proper website, CRM (HubSpot or similar), and runs Google Ads.

**Pain points:**
- Existing lead gen platforms (Bark, Checkatrade) generate volume but quality is inconsistent — lots of "just researching" leads.
- Managing multiple cert renewal dates across solar, ASHP, and EV lines is admin-heavy.
- Wants to understand which product lines are most profitable — does reporting by job type.
- Needs to manage which team members see which jobs and who can quote.

**What would make them sign up:**
- Multi-user team access (they need admin to log in, fitters to see jobs, directors to see P&L).
- Coverage area control by product — they might do solar across 10 postcode districts but ASHP only in 3.
- Integration potential: API or CSV export so they can push jobs into their CRM.
- Referral fee is not the blocker — they care more about lead quality and conversion rate.

**What would make them churn:**
- Poor match rate — receiving solar briefs when they want ASHP.
- No way to turn off a product line temporarily (e.g. ASHP fitter leaves the team).
- Friction if a team member leaves and the account is tied to one login.
- Slow payment release from WattSmart after installation.

### Persona C — The ASHP Specialist

**Size:** 2–8 people, often spun out of a plumbing or HVAC background. May have only recently added MCS to an existing business. Turnover ~£300k–£800k.

**Typical setup:** MCS certified for ASHP, often F-Gas too. May be NAPIT, Gas Safe if they came from heating. Relationship with Worcester Bosch, Mitsubishi, or Vaillant as an approved installer.

**Pain points:**
- ASHP market is still maturing — lots of customer confusion about suitability. Hates doing surveys that lead nowhere because the property isn't a good fit.
- BUS (Boiler Upgrade Scheme) admin adds overhead — needs a platform that understands the grant flow.
- Competing with large national boiler companies that have branched into heat pumps.
- Customers often compare them to gas boiler quotes — price education is exhausting.

**What would make them sign up:**
- Brief quality: does the customer already understand they want an ASHP and know their property type? If WattSmart filters out unsuitable properties (solid walls, no loft insulation) before sending the brief, this is a major selling point.
- BUS grant processing compatibility — can the platform handle the staged payment (grant paid to installer)?
- Being seen as a vetted specialist, not just a builder on a list.

**What would make them churn:**
- Leads for properties that are clearly unsuitable for ASHP (no mention of EPC rating or property type in the brief).
- Win rate below ~20% — ASHP quotes take longer to prepare, so a low conversion feels expensive in time.
- Being matched against solar-only companies that have ticked "heat pumps" opportunistically.

### Persona D — The EV Charger Installer

**Size:** Typically an electrician running a 1–3 person business. May have diversified into EV chargers as a new revenue stream. Turnover £80k–£250k.

**Typical setup:** NICEIC or NAPIT registered, OZEV-authorised. Often does domestic EV alongside commercial OZEV work (car parks, fleets).

**Pain points:**
- EV charger jobs are shorter and lower value (avg. £800–£1,200 domestic) compared to solar. Needs volume.
- Charger brand choices (Ohme, Zappi, Hypervolt) affect profit margins — dislikes being asked to supply a specific brand without prior agreement.
- Domestic OZEV grant means they often deal with multiple grant applications per day. More admin than any other product.
- Gets direct enquiries from car dealerships trying to pass customers to installers for free.

**What would make them sign up:**
- Volume: if WattSmart can provide consistent EV lead flow, the low fee per job is fine.
- A clear signal in the brief about which charger brand the customer has in mind (or "no preference").
- Speed: EV customers want their charger installed fast. Seeing brief-to-quote deadlines helps.

**What would make them churn:**
- Quoting on briefs that go quiet — customer changed their mind after getting delivery date for the car.
- Low brief frequency if demand in their postcode area is thin.
- Any admin that delays getting paid — their margin per job is tight.

### Persona E — The Established Multi-Trade with Solar as Upsell

**Size:** 10–50 employees. Core business is building, roofing, or electrical contracting. Solar added as a complementary service. Turnover £2M–£15M.

**Typical setup:** MCS for solar and battery, possibly EV. Has scaffolding capability. May employ a dedicated solar project manager.

**Pain points:**
- Solar is not the core product — it competes for fitter time with more profitable main contracts.
- Doesn't want to be locked into solar-only lead platforms. Wants flexibility to pause.
- Finance/procurement team is cautious about new supplier relationships.
- Brand-conscious: won't want to appear on a platform they perceive as "budget" leads.

**What would make them sign up:**
- Premium positioning of WattSmart: "vetted lead marketplace" not "job board."
- Ability to pause lead flow easily (holidays, capacity constraints) without penalty.
- A genuine service level agreement on lead quality.

**What would make them churn:**
- Mismatch between lead quality perception and reality.
- Feeling like a small fish on a platform dominated by sole traders quoting low.

## Part 2: Channels to Reach MCS-Certified Installers

### Priority 1 — Direct from MCS Register

The MCS Contractor Directory is public at mcscertified.com. It includes company name, postcode, and product scopes. This is the highest-quality prospecting list available and is freely searchable.

- **Approach:** Scrape or manually extract company names and postcodes by product type and region. Cross-reference with Companies House to get director contact details (required for cold email). This is fully legal under B2B email rules (PECR soft opt-in).
- **Volume:** ~4,500 MCS-certified companies as of 2025.
- **Best for:** All persona types. Filter by product scope and postcode to target exactly.

### Priority 2 — Trade Associations (Warm Outreach via Membership)

| Association | Members | Contact route |
|---|---|---|
| MCS | ~4,500 contractors | Via their newsletter/directory — approach partnership team |
| RECC | ~700 members | Membership newsletter partnership |
| HIES | ~600 members | Similar to RECC |
| NAPIT | ~10,000 registered businesses | Partnership/advertising in NAPIT Contractor magazine |
| TrustMark | ~20,000 registered tradespeople | Advertise in TrustMark bulletin or approach scheme operator |

A formal MCS partnership (endorsed listing or newsletter placement) would be the highest-trust entry point and could drive hundreds of warm registrations. Approach their membership team with a joint value proposition: WattSmart helps MCS contractors win more qualified work.

### Priority 3 — Trade Press

These publications are read by installation business owners, not just engineers.

- **Solar Power Portal** (solarpowerportal.co.uk) — UK's leading solar trade publication. Banner ads, sponsored articles, or a "Installer Partner" feature. Reaches solar and battery installers.
- **HVP Magazine** (hvpmag.co.uk) — Heating, Ventilation, Plumbing. Core readership for ASHP and heat pump installers.
- **The Installer** (theinstaller.co.uk) — Monthly print and digital. Broad HVAC and renewables reach.
- **Electrical Times / Electrical Review** — Reaches electricians moving into EV chargers.
- **Solar & Storage Live** — Annual trade show at NEC Birmingham. Stand + co-marketing with MCS.

Suggested format: a half-page or full-page ad in Solar Power Portal focused on "Join WattSmart — quality leads, no upfront cost." Plus a contributed article or podcast spot pitched to the editorial team.

### Priority 4 — LinkedIn

LinkedIn is effective for reaching directors and owners of installation businesses, especially multi-tech companies (Persona B and E).

- **Search filters:** Job title "Director" OR "Owner" + company size 2–50 + industry "Renewables & Environment" or "Electrical/Electronic Manufacturing."
- **Group targeting:** UK Solar Energy Society, Renewable Energy Jobs & Industry (UK), Heat Pump Association Forum (informal).
- **Content strategy:** Post case studies ("an installer in the NE won 4 jobs this quarter through WattSmart") and comparison posts ("why qualified leads beat job boards"). LinkedIn article series on running a profitable solar business.
- **InMail budget:** £0.50–£1 per credit. Use only after a content warm-up sequence — cold InMail is weak.

### Priority 5 — Facebook Groups

Sole traders (Persona A) are heavily active on Facebook.

- **UK Solar Industry** — active group, ~3,000 members, mix of installers and product suppliers.
- **MCS Installers UK** — smaller but highly targeted.
- **Renewable Energy Professionals UK** — broader but reaches ASHP and heat pump fitters.
- **Approach:** Join as a WattSmart team member, contribute genuinely (answer questions about certifications, lead quality) before posting promotional content. Sponsored posts in these groups are possible via Meta Business but targeting is imprecise.
